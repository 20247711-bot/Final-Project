Adobo: The Dish That Tells a Story

A staple of Filipino cuisine, a history of preservation.

The question of "who invented Adobo" doesn't point to a single chef or date, but rather to the ingenuity of ancient, indigenous Filipinos.

Their solution: cooking meat in a mixture dominated by vinegar and salt, creating a natural preservation method that predates refrigeration.

The Name: A Colonial Label

Spanish colonizers, including Friar Pedro de San Buenaventura, named it after the Spanish word adobar, meaning “to marinate.”

By 1613, Buenaventura recorded “adobos de los naturales”, a term that stayed even as the Filipino dish evolved through Chinese influences like soy sauce (toyo).

A rich, savory, and tangy Filipino staple best served with rice.

Ingredients

  • 1 lb Pork shoulder/belly
  • 1 lb Chicken pieces
  • 1/2 cup Soy Sauce
  • 1/2 cup White Vinegar
  • 1 cup Water
  • 1 head Garlic, crushed
  • 3 Bay Leaves
  • 1 tsp Peppercorns
  • 1 tsp Brown Sugar (optional)
  • 2 tbsp Cooking Oil

Instructions

  1. Marinate: Combine meat with soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, peppercorns, and bay leaves.
  2. Sauté: Brown the garlic and meat.
  3. Simmer: Add marinade and water, then boil.
  4. Braise: Cover and simmer for up to 1 hour.
  5. Reduce: Uncover and thicken the sauce.
  6. Serve: Best with hot rice.

TUTORIAL


Tip: Let the vinegar boil before lowering the heat to mellow the sourness.
