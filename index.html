Adobo

Adobo is arguably the most famous and beloved Filipino dish. It involves meat (usually chicken, pork, or a combination) braised in savory sauce made of *soy sauce*, *vinegar*, *crushed garlic*, and *black peppercorns*. The cooking method, which preserves the meat, was a clever technique used long before refrigeration.

Sinigang

Sinigang is the quintessential Filipino sour soup. Its distinctive tangy flavor comes from a souring agent, most commonly *tamarind (sampaloc)*, but sometimes using guava, calamansi, or even unripe mango. It’s packed with protein (pork, beef, shrimp, or fish) and a vibrant mix of vegetables like taro, tomatoes, okra, radish, and water spinach (kangkong).

Tinola

Tinola is a Filipino chicken soup known for its light, gingery broth. It’s cooked with chicken simmered in ginger, garlic, and onions, then paired with green papaya or sayote and finished with fresh malunggay leaves. Simple, aromatic, and nourishing, tinola is often enjoyed as a comforting home-style dish.

Afritada

Afritada is a Filipino tomato-based stew made with chicken or pork simmered in a rich, savory tomato sauce. It features classic vegetables like potatoes, carrots, and bell peppers, creating a hearty, colorful mix. Mildly sweet and deeply flavorful, afritada is a well-loved everyday dish served with plenty of rice.



Bibingka

A soft rice cake traditionally cooked in clay pots and topped with butter, salted egg, and cheese, usually enjoyed during Christmas season.

Puto

Steamed rice cakes that are lightly sweet, often paired with dinuguan or eaten as a snack.

Kakanin

Sticky rice wrapped in banana leaves, sometimes served with latik or sugar.

Leche Flan

A rich, creamy caramel custard dessert made with eggs, milk, and sugar.

Halo-Halo

A cold dessert with crushed ice, milk, sweetened fruits, jellies, beans, and topped with leche flan or ube ice cream.
